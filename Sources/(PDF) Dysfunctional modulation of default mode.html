Dysfunctional modulation of default mode network activity in attention-deficit/hyperactivity disorder

Eric Achten2015, Journal of Abnormal Psychology https://doi.org/10.1037/ABN0000013visibility … description 7 pages

Abstract

The state regulation deficit model posits that individuals with attention-deficit/hyperactivity disorder (ADHD) have difficulty applying mental effort effectively under suboptimal conditions such as very fast and very slow event rates (ERs). ADHD is also associated with diminished suppression of default mode network (DMN) activity and related performance deficits on tasks requiring effortful engagement. The current study builds on these 2 literatures to test the hypothesis that failure to modulate DMN activity in ADHD might be especially pronounced at ER extremes. Nineteen adults with ADHD and 20 individuals without any neuropsychiatric condition successfully completed a simple target detection task under 3 ER conditions (2-, 4-, and 8-s interstimulus intervals) inside the scanner. Task-related DMN deactivations were compared between 2 groups. There was a differential effect of ER on DMN activity for individuals with ADHD compared to controls. Individuals with ADHD displayed excessive DMN activity at the fast and slow, but not at the moderate ER. The results indicate that DMN attenuation in ADHD is disrupted in suboptimal energetic states where additional effort is required to optimize task engagement. DMN dysregulation may be an important element of the neurobiological underpinnings of state regulation deficits in ADHD. Key takeaways AI

  1. ADHD individuals exhibit excessive DMN activity at fast and slow event rates, confirming the state regulation deficit model.
  2. The study involved 19 adults with ADHD and 20 controls across three event rate conditions: 2, 4, and 8 seconds.
  3. DMN deactivation fails in ADHD at suboptimal energetic states, leading to task engagement deficits.
  4. Significant interaction between group and event rate (p=0.002) indicates differential DMN modulation in ADHD.
  5. Future research should explore neurobiological interactions between DMN and other regulatory brain networks.
