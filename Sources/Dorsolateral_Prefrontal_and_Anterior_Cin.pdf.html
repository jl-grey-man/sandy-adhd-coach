Dorsolateral Prefrontal and Anterior Cingulate Cortex Volumetric Abnormalities in Adults with Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder Identified by Magnetic Resonance Imaging

Larry J. Seidman, Eve M. Valera, Nikos Makris, Michael C. Monuteaux, Denise L. Boriel, Kalika Kelkar, David N. Kennedy, Verne S. Caviness, George Bush, Meg Aleardi, Stephen V. Faraone, and Joseph Biederman Objectives: Gray and white matter volume deficits have been reported in a number of studies of children with attention-deficit/ hyperactivity disorder (ADHD); however, there is a paucity of structural magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) studies of adults with ADHD. This structural MRI study used an a priori region of interest approach. Methods: Twenty-four adults with DSM-IV ADHD and 18 healthy controls comparable on age, socioeconomic status, sex, handedness, education, IQ, and achievement test performance had an MRI on a 1.5T Siemens scanner. Cortical and sub-cortical gray and white matter were segmented. Image parcellation divided the neocortex into 48 gyral-based units per hemisphere. Based on a priori hypotheses we focused on prefrontal, anterior cingulate cortex (ACC) and overall gray matter volumes. General linear analyses of the volumes of brain regions, adjusting for age, sex, and total cerebral volumes, were used to compare groups. Results: Relative to controls, ADHD adults had significantly smaller overall cortical gray matter, prefrontal and ACC volumes. Conclusions: Adults with ADHD have volume differences in brain regions in areas involved in attention and executive control. These data, largely consistent with studies of children, support the idea that adults with ADHD have a valid disorder with persistent biological features. Key Words: Attention-deficit/hyperactivity disorder, anterior cingulate, prefrontal cortex, structural MRI

ost of what is known about attention-deficit/hyperactiv-ity disorder (ADHD) has come from studies of children. Nonetheless, over the past decade, adult ADHD has gradually become recognized as a valid and reliable disorder that shares many features with the childhood form (Faraone et al 2000). Approximately 30 – 60% of individuals diagnosed with ADHD in youth have symptoms that persist into adulthood (Biederman 1998; Biederman et al 2000) and epidemiological data indicates that approximately 4% of adults in the USA are afflicted with ADHD (Kessler et al 2006). Adults with ADHD share similar clinical features, comorbidities, neuropsychological deficits, and failures in major life domains with ADHD children (Faraone et al 2000; Hechtman 1992; Mannuzza et al 1993; Weiss and Hechtman 1986). Little is known about the neuroanatomy of ADHD in adults (Faraone 2004). Whereas there are at least 27 published quanti-tative reports of structural neuroimaging using magnetic reso-nance imaging (MRI) in ADHD children from more than a dozen research groups (Seidman et al 2005), there is only one published structural imaging study of adults (Hesslinger et al 2002). That study evaluated differences in total cerebral volume between eight ADHD and 17 healthy males. Their only positive finding was a significant reduction of the left orbitofrontal cortex (OFC). However, given the small sample, these preliminary results require replication and extension. Current conceptualizations of ADHD have hypothesized that ADHD involves structural and functional brain abnormalities in frontal-striatal circuitry (Castellanos 1997; Durston 2003; Faraone 2004; Tannock 1998). We have reviewed this literature qualita-tively (Seidman et al 2004, 2005) and have conducted a meta-analysis of morphometric studies (Valera et al, unpublished data), providing convergent support for this hypothesis. Recent studies using automated voxel-based morphometry (VBM) or surface density techniques (Sowell et al 2003b) provide more support for this hypothesis, but they also suggest that the brain is altered in a more widespread manner than has been previously hypothesized. The most replicated alterations in ADHD in childhood include significantly smaller volumes in the prefrontal cortex (PFC), caudate, splenium of the corpus callosum, cerebellum, and overall cerebral volume. The studies assessed boys between the ages of 4 and 18 (with a mean age of approximately 11) but the few studies that include girls are largely consistent with these findings (Castellanos et al 2001, 2002). Thus, along with theoret-ical models about the pathophysiology of ADHD, empirical data from ADHD children can be used to predict possible abnormal-ities in adults. Prefrontal hypotheses of ADHD have primarily involved the dorsolateral PFC (DLPFC) and OFC. Given the strong evidence of executive function deficits and dopaminergic alterations in ADHD (Castellanos 1997; Faraone 2004; Tannock 1998), many investigators hypothesize DLPFC dysfunction in the disorder (Barkley 1997). All of the studies measuring at least one com-partment of PFC reported smaller volumes in ADHD children. Nine volumetric MRI reports of ADHD children (Castellanos et al 1996, 2001, 2002; Durston et al 2004; Filipek et al 1997; Hill et al 2003; Hynd et al 1990; Kates et al 2002; Mostofsky et al 2002) identified smaller volumes in areas corresponding to left or right DLPFC. Overmeyer et al (2001), using VBM showed a reduction in right superior frontal gyrus volume. Sowell et al (2003b) reported reduced brain surface extent in inferior portions of DLPFC bilaterally using computational image analyses. The ACC, especially the dorsal ACC, has strong connections to the DLPFC and is considered to play a critical role in complex cognitive processing (Bush et al 2000), particularly target detection, response selection, error detection, and reward-based decision-making (Bush et al 2002), functions that are thought to be impaired in ADHD. There is one VBM study reporting significant cingulate abnormalities in ADHD children showing a reduction in right posterior cingulate (Overmeyer et al 2001). Kates et al (2002) failed to show a difference in total cingulate volume, while Mostofsky et al (2002) failed to show a difference in anterior cingulate tissue. The caudate, putamen, and globus pallidus (“pallidum”) are part of discrete, somatotopically distributed circuits essential for executive functions, including prefrontal-basal ganglia-thalamic loops (Alexander et al 1986). Damage to the striatum has been hypothesized to be associated with the etiology of ADHD (Lou 1996). Experimental striatal lesions in animals produce hyperac-tivity and poor working memory and response inhibition perfor-mance (Alexander et al 1986), and the striatum has one of the highest densities of dopaminergic synapses (Dougherty et al 1999). Finally, stimulant medications, commonly used to treat ADHD, increase synaptic dopamine by blocking the dopamine transporter in the striatum (Volkow et al 2002). Nine of 13 studies have shown significantly smaller caudate total or the head alone, either on the left or right side (Castellanos et al 1994, 1996, 2001, 2002, 2003; Filipek et al 1997; Hynd et al 1993; Mataro et al 1997; Semrud-Clikeman et al 1994). Recently, Castellanos et al (2002) demonstrated that significant differences in caudate volume between children with ADHD and controls diminished by the oldest age studied (19 years). This was the only region that showed a “normalization” of brain volume over time. This suggests that studies of ADHD adults are needed to determine the persistence and stability of anatomical alterations. The nucleus accumbens (NA), while not previously studied in ADHD, may be important, because of its essential role in reward mechanisms (Blum et al 1996). Alterations in reward behavior have been consistently reported in ADHD children (Sonuga-Barke 2005) and they might account for their increased risk for substance use disorders (Biederman et al 1997). Because the NA has also been linked to impulsivity (Cardinal et al 2004; King et al 2003) as well as reward in ADHD, the NA is a relevant candidate region of interest (ROI) for the disorder (Seidman et al 2004). Although the cerebellum has traditionally been thought to be primarily involved in motor control, clinical and research find-ings over the past 20 years have shown cerebellar involvement in a number of cognitive and affective processes (Schmahmann and Sherman 1998). Moreover, Middleton and Strick (2001) have demonstrated cerebellar-cortical connections that provide an anatomical substrate for a possible cerebellar-prefrontal dysfunc-tion in ADHD. All five groups of researchers studying the cerebellum in ADHD children have observed structural abnor-malities, including volume reduction in specific regions of the vermis. For example, reductions in the posterior inferior lobules, VIII to X, of the cerebellar vermis have been found for both ADHD boys (Berquin et al 1998; Bussing et al 2002; Castellanos et al 1996; Hill et al 2003; Mostofsky et al 1998) and girls (Castellanos et al 2001; Hill et al 2003). Durston et al (2004) found smaller overall right cerebellar volumes in a group of 30 ADHD children. In the largest study, of 152 children and adolescents with ADHD and 139 matched controls (Castellanos et al 2002), after adjusting for total cerebral volume, only the difference for the cerebellar volume remained significant. Additionally, cere-bellar volumes were significantly and negatively correlated with ratings of attentional problems. Seven of 12 reports (Castellanos et al 1996, 2001, 2002; Filipek et al 1997; Hill et al 2003; Kates et al 2002; Mostofsky et al 2002) have shown that in ADHD children the total cerebrum—partic-ularly the right hemisphere—is smaller by 3% to 5%, and one study reported reduced intracranial volume (Durston et al 2004). Several studies also reported smaller total gray and white matter (Castellanos et al 2002; Mostofsky et al 2002). No study reported significantly larger volumes. Based on this prior work, we hypothesized that ADHD adults would have smaller volumes in prefrontal (especially DLPFC) cortex, ACC, overall brain volume, overall gray matter volumes, and cerebellar gray matter. Second, we explored whether the nucleus accumbens was significantly different in ADHD than in controls, but we did not have a directional hypothesis. Third, based on Castellanos et al (2002), we predicted that there would not be a difference in the caudate. Finally, we explored a range of other volumes measured from the general segmentation and cortical parcellation analyses. Methods and Materials Subjects Males and females between the ages of 18 and 59 were eligible for the study. ADHD (n $$ 24) and control (n $$ 18) adults were group matched to be comparable on age, socioeconomic status (SES), sex distribution, handedness, and education (Table 1). Functional imaging data using most of these subjects has been published (Valera et al 2005); this is the first report of the structural imaging data. Exclusion criteria were deafness, blind-ness, psychosis, neurological disorder, sensorimotor handicaps, inadequate command of the English language, or a Full Scale intelligence quotient (IQ) estimate less than 80 as measured by the Wechsler Adult Intelligence Scale-Revised (WAIS-R; Wechsler 1981). SES was measured by the Hollingshead scale (1975). No ethnic or racial group was excluded. We recruited ADHD subjects from referrals to psychiatric clinics at the Massachusetts General Hospital (MGH) and advertisements in the greater Boston area, and control subjects through advertisements in the same geographical areas. After complete description of the study to the subjects, written informed consent was obtained, and all participants received an honorarium for participating. The study was approved by the MGH Human Subjects IRB committee. Clinical Assessment Measures ADHD adults were only included if they met full criteria for current ADHD according to the DSM-IV, with childhood onset and persistence into adulthood. To our knowledge, none of these participants had been treated before age 18. Of the 24 persons with ADHD, nine were Inattentive, two were Hyperac-tive-Impulsive, and 12 had combined sub-type (one subject’s data was missing). We conducted direct interviews with all subjects. Trained lay interviewers, blind to ascertainment status, interviewed all adults with the Structured Clinical Interview for DSM-IV (First et al 1997) supplemented with modules from the Kiddie SADS-E (Orvaschel 1994) to cover ADHD and other childhood disorders. Before interviewing research participants, interviewers completed a 4-month training program that in-cluded mastery of the instruments, learning DSM-IV criteria, watching training tapes, observing interviews performed by experienced raters, rating several subjects under the supervision of the project coordinator, and completing practice interviews. Throughout the study, they were supervised by board-certified child and adolescent psychiatrists and experienced licensed psychologists in weekly meetings. During the study, all inter-views were audiotaped for random quality control assessments. The interviewers had been instructed to take extensive notes about the symptoms for each disorder. These notes and the structured interview data were reviewed by the diagnostic com-mittee so that the Committee could make a Best Estimate diagnosis as described by Leckman et al (1982). Initial diagnoses were prepared by the study interviewers and were then reviewed by a Diagnostic Committee of board-certified child and adoles-cent psychiatrists and experienced licensed psychologists. The Diagnostic Committee was blind to the subject’s ascertainment group, all data collected from other family members, and all non-diagnostic (e.g., brain imaging) data. Diagnoses were made for two points in time: lifetime and past month. Diagnoses were considered definite only if a consensus was achieved that criteria were met to a degree that would be considered clinically meaningful. By “clinically meaningful” we mean that the data collected from the structured interview indicated that the diag-nosis should be a clinical concern due to the nature of the symptoms, the associated impairment, and the coherence of the clinical picture. We computed kappa coefficients of diagnostic agreement by having experienced, board certified child and adult psychiatrists diagnose subjects from audio taped interviews. Based on 500 assessments from interviews of children and adults, the median kappa coefficient was .98. Kappa coefficients for individual diag-noses included: ADHD (.88), conduct disorder (1.0), major depres-sion (1.0), mania (.95), separation anxiety (1.0), agoraphobia (1.0), panic (.95), substance use disorder (1.0), and tics/Tourette’s (.89). A neuropsychological battery was also administered. For this paper we include estimates of IQ, academic achievement and rates of learning disability (LD). An IQ was estimated from the Block Design and Vocabulary subtests of the WAIS-R (Wechsler 1981). Academic achievement was assessed with the Reading and Arithmetic tests of the Wide Range Achievement Test – Revised (Jastak and Jastak 1985). A sagittal localizer scan was per-formed for placement of slices, followed by a coronal T2-weighted sequence to rule out unexpected neuropathology. Two sagittal 3D MP-RAGE (T1-weighted, non-selective inversion-prepared spoiled gradient echo pulse) sequences were collected (TR/TE/T1/flip $$ 2.73 s/3.39 ms/1.0 s/7, bandwidth $$ 190 Hz/pixel, sampling matrix $$ 256 $$ 192 pixels, FOV $$ 256 $$ 256 mm, effective slice thickness $$ 1.33 mm on a 170 mm slab of 128 partitions) and used for morphometric analyses conducted at the MGH Center for Morphometric Analysis (CMA). Individuals with ADHD using psychostimulants were asked to refrain from medications for 24 hours prior to scanning (for the functional MRI protocol that was collected at the same session). Image Analysis Structural scans were transferred to the CMA and coded and cataloged for blind analysis. Imaging analysis was done on workstations with Cardviews software (Caviness et al 1996). Gray and White Matter Whole Brain Segmentation. Brain images were positionally normalized to overcome variations in head position by using a standard 3-dimensional coordinate system on each scan. This procedure uses the midpoints of the decussations of the anterior and posterior commissure lines and the midsagittal plane at the level of the posterior commissure as points of reference for rotation and translation. This ‘self-refer-ential’ system is based directly on the individual brain, and is not warped to a template. The datasets were then segmented into gray, white, and cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) tissue classes using a semi-automated intensity contour algorithm for external border definition and signal intensity histogram distributions for delin-eation of gray-white borders. This technique, described in detail elsewhere (Filipek et al 1989; Kennedy et al 1989), yields separate components of neocortex, subcortical gray nuclei, white matter, and ventricular system that correspond to the natural tissue boundaries distinguished by signal intensities on T1-weighted images. ROIs included total cerebrum, cortical gray and white matter, lateral, third and fourth ventricles, caudate, puta-men, pallidum, nucleus accumbens, hippocampus, amygdala, thalamus, brainstem, cerebellar cortex, and cerebellar central mass (including cerebellar white matter and deep nuclei). Parcellation of the Neocortex. The neocortex, as defined by the segmentation procedure, was then divided into 48 parcella-tion units (PUs) per hemisphere, based on the system originally described by Rademacher et al (1992) and modified by Caviness and colleagues (1997). This is a comprehensive system of neocortical subdivision designed to approximate architectonic and functional subdivisions, and based on specific anatomical landmarks present in all brains (Rademacher et al 1993). The CMA collaborators (Drs. Caviness, Kennedy, and Makris), devel-oped these procedures, trained the technicians, and maintained quality control for the segmentation and parcellation of the data. All morphometric measurements were performed blind to diag-nostic status. The first author has experience with these proce-dures with other psychiatric disorders (Goldstein et al 1999). Volumes were calculated for each PU by multiplying the area measurement of the PU on each slice by the slice thickness, followed by summing across all slices in which the PU appears. The PUs were combined into clusters based on lobar and connectivity data into cortical volumes of the frontal, parietal, temporal, and occipital lobes. Medial paralimbic and limbic regions were also formed as part of the groupings (Goldstein et al 1999). Our hypotheses focused on PFC and ACC in particular. To further investigate the anatomy of alterations of the ACC, based on hypotheses by Bush that the dorsal ACC in particular is abnormal in ADHD (Bush et al 2000, 2002), we used a novel method to produce isosurface differences. The 24 participants with ADHD and 18 matched control subjects were registered (aligned) to a reference brain that was separate from either cohort. ACC probability for each group was calculated on a voxel-by-voxel basis with the aligned data. Isosurfaces for the probability .5 were created for each group separately. Three-dimensional visualization of these surfaces was used to look for systematic differences in the topology of the ACC between groups. Data Analyses First, we compared the ADHD and control groups on demo- graphic, psychiatric, and cognitive factors, using the chi-square test, Wilcoxon rank sum test and Student t test for categorical, ordinal, and dimensional variables, respectively. Next, we esti-mated two sets of models, each with the complete array of volumetric outcomes as the dependent variables. In the first set of models, we estimated the outcomes as a function of ADHD status, gender, and age, and compared the results to simple Student t tests (the results did not differ systematically and we do not present them). In the second set of models, we estimated the outcomes as a function of ADHD status, gender, age, and either total brain volume (e.g., the sum of the volumes of the total cerebrum, the total cerebellum, the third and fourth ventricles, and the brain stem) or total cerebral volume, depending on the ROI (see Tables 2–4). All models were estimated using linear regression. All tests were two-tailed and alpha was set at .05 for a priori hypotheses (e.g., DLPFC and ACC) and .01 for explor-atory analyses (e.g., NA). We present the percent volume differ-ence as the mean of the controls minus the mean of ADHD divided by the mean of the controls. For each comparison, an effect size (d) (Cohen 1988) is also presented: mean of controls minus the mean of ADHD divided by the pooled standard deviation. Using Cohen’s convention, effect sizes were consid-ered small (d $$ .20), medium (d $$ .50) and large (d $$ .80 or greater). Results Demographic Characteristics, Intellectual Functioning, and Symptoms As Table 1 shows, compared with non-ADHD adults, adults with ADHD were not significantly different on age, SES, sex distribution, handedness, or education. All subjects were Cauca-sian. The groups were statistically comparable on IQ, reading and arithmetic achievement, and frequency of LDs (which was low in both groups). Both groups were highly educated and were above average in general intellectual ability. There were no significant differences between groups on lifetime rates of mood, anxiety, combined substance, or antisocial disorders. While the groups were comparable on alcohol use, persons with ADHD had higher lifetime rates of substance abuse (29% vs. 0%). There were no group differences in use of psychotropic medications at the time of the MRI scan (one person in each group was taking an anti-depressant), nor were there differences a year earlier when originally recruited into Dr. Faraone’s study. General Segmentation There were no significant differences in the whole brain or total cerebral volume. Adults with ADHD had significantly smaller neocortex (i.e., cortical gray matter) and significantly greater white matter volumes (Figure 1). These differences were approximately 6.5%, representing medium effect sizes (Table 2). Regarding the basal ganglia, there were no significant differences for the caudate, putamen or pallidum, but the nucleus accum-bens was 9.1% larger in the adults with ADHD. There were no significant differences for the amygdala, hippocampus, thalamus, cerebellum, brainstem, or ventricles. These data point to the cortex as the primary volumetric difference. We followed up these findings by evaluating the four cortical lobes, and medial paralimbic and limbic regions. Cortical Parcellation. All cortical regions were smaller in ADHD. However, only the frontal lobe (8.3%, d $$ .61) and medial paralimbic areas were significantly smaller in adults with ADHD (Table 3). This was accounted for by significantly smaller left and right frontal lobes, and a significantly smaller right medial paralimbic region (8.0%, d $$ .58). Percent volume reduction in the other ROIs ranged from 5.1 to 7.6% except for the temporal lobe, which was only 2.8% smaller. We further investigated frontal and medial paralimbic regions based on a priori hypotheses. We found the most substantial volumetric differences in the ACC (13.9%) and the superior frontal gyrus (11.0%) that were significantly smaller in ADHD. The ACC was significantly smaller in the right hemisphere (Table 4). T h e superior frontal gyrus (“F1”), which in the CMA system is comprised of approximately 60% DLPFC, was significant for the left hemisphere (Figure 2). Discussion In the first volumetric study of the brain in adults with ADHD, we found substantial support for the hypothesis that adults with ADHD have structural brain abnormalities in a priori predicted regions of interest (ACC, DLPFC, and overall cortical gray matter) that were based on a literature review (Seidman et al 2005) and a meta-analysis of studies in ADHD children and teenagers (Valera et al, unpublished data). These results extend to adults with ADHD previously reported findings in the pediatric litera-ture on children with ADHD documenting abnormalities in critical brain regions associated with the regulation of attention and executive function. These biological data provide strong support for the validity of ADHD in adults and for the persistence into adult life of biological features found in children with ADHD. It is striking that abnormalities were detectable in the absence of any significant differences on demographic, psychiatric, or cognitive variables used to group match. Both groups were highly educated, were above average in general intellectual ability, and had very infrequent LDs. Moreover, there were no significant differences between groups on lifetime rates of mood, anxiety, combined substance, or antisocial disorders. Thus, the significant brain volume reductions in the ADHD group can be attributed to having ADHD, and not to co-occurring psychiatric or cognitive comorbidity. This is a critical demonstration, as it has not been previously known whether the hypothesized brain volume abnormalities in adults with ADHD are due to ADHD or to the various common comorbidities also known to have associated structural brain abnormalities. The only previous MRI study of adults with ADHD was conducted by Hesslinger and colleagues (2002). In that study, the investigators compared eight ADHD and 17 healthy male adults. As in our study, they failed to find differences in total cerebral volume. They used an ROI analysis to examine the OFC based on the hypothesis of potential affective dysregulation in ADHD individuals. Their finding of a significant volume reduction of the left OFC confirmed their hypothesis. In contrast, we found no OFC volume reductions in adult ADHD. More work is needed to clarify this difference. Comparatively little has been published about the neuro-anatomy of ADHD in adults in contrast to children (Seidman et al 2005). Despite the obvious paucity of structural imaging studies of adults with the disorder, our findings are largely consistent with the pediatric literature, especially with respect to significant reductions in overall and prefrontal gray matter volume (Seidman et al 2005; Valera et al, unpublished data). Seidman et al www.sobp.org/journal The differences between adults with ADHD and healthy comparisons in the NA and white matter are novel and bear further discussion. Although we consider these findings margin-ally significant trends, as the direction was not predicted, and the significance levels did not achieve p $$ .01 as we required, the NA may be important in adults with ADHD, because of its essential role in reward mechanisms (Blum et al 1996). Alterations in reward behavior have been considered by some to be critically abnormal in ADHD (Sonuga-Barke 2005). The NA has been shown to receive substantial inputs from the ACC. Through outputs to the substantia nigra and the ventral pallidum, the NA gains access to the motor system and becomes a crucial part of a ventral-striatal loop (Aggleton et al 1987). On the basis of this anatomy it has been suggested that the NA may be important for the association of motivationally and emotionally salient stimuli with motor responses and may also be involved in behavioral switching or cognitive flexibility, all dysfunctions associated with ADHD. Because dysregulated reward mechanisms have been linked to ADHD, we believe that our preliminary finding may be meaningful and needs to be followed up and replicated. The finding of significantly increased white matter in the ADHD adults is discrepant with studies of children by Castella-nos et al (2002), who reported significantly reduced white matter in ADHD. A number of possibilities can be considered to explain these different findings: 1) Sample differences: Our sample included individuals with relatively high IQ and low psychiatric comorbidity, especially antisocial disorders, suggesting that it may represent a higher functioning tail of the ADHD distribution; 2) The finding may reflect different developmental processes across the lifespan in adults with ADHD. In the latter case, this result may be a relative “imbalance” in gray-white matter devel-opment in which there is excessive growth of white matter in ADHD. Because white matter continues to develop until the late 20s or older (Gogtay et al 2004; Sowell et al 2003a), some genetic process that regulates white matter development may be altered in ADHD. More work is needed to further examine these speculations. The absence of a significant difference in the caudate (d $$ .20) is consistent with previous work in older as compared to younger ADHD teenagers (Castellanos et al 2002), suggesting a “normalization” of the caudate with age in ADHD. More research with older teenagers and adults is necessary before we accept this developmental hypothesis. The comparisons of cerebellar volumes demonstrated nonsig-nificantly smaller volumes (medium effect sizes) in ADHD, although they could be statistically significant if the 5% reduction in cerebellar gray matter is found with a larger sample. We will subsequently test the cerebellar hypothesis with a larger sample, and also parcellate cerebellar subdivisions previously found to be significantly smaller in children with ADHD (i. e., vermis) using a cerebellar parcellation tool developed at the CMA (Makris et al 2005). Conclusions and Future Directions Despite these limitations, the results provide support for the hypothesis that structural brain abnormalities persist into adult-hood in ADHD, thus complementing studies demonstrating neuropsychological and functional brain dysfunctions in adults with ADHD (Bush et al 2005; Seidman in press). Replication of these findings in adults is necessary to establish that the partic-ular structures found to be abnormal are core components of the neurobiology of ADHD. Since both genetic factors (Faraone et al 2005) and obstetric complications (Milberger et al 1998) have been suggested as risk factors for ADHD, it is crucial to investi-gate the possibility that either independent and interactive aspects of these causes may result in the brain abnormalities. Moreover, while we are currently assuming that these volumetric differences in ADHD are independent of treatment (Castellanos et al 2001), it is at least theoretically possible that, like behavioral or cognitive improvements with stimulant treatment, volumetric differences may be modified by treatment interventions. The neuroanatomical and neurophysiological study of adults with ADHD is in its infancy, and many new exciting directions can be anticipated.
