Research Review: Dopamine transfer deficit: a neurobiological theory of altered reinforcement mechanisms in ADHD

Gail Tripp1,2 and Jeff R. Wickens1,3 1Okinawa Institute of Science and Technology, Okinawa, Japan; 2Department of Psychology, 3Department of Anatomy & Structural Biology, University of Otago, Dunedin, New Zealand This review considers the hypothesis that changes in dopamine signalling might account for altered sensitivity to positive reinforcement in children with ADHD. The existing evidence regarding dopamine cell activity in relation to positive reinforcement is reviewed. We focus on the anticipatory firing of dopamine cells brought about by a transfer of dopamine cell responses to cues that precede reinforcers. It is proposed that in children with ADHD there is diminished anticipatory dopamine cell firing, which we call the dopamine transfer deficit (DTD). The DTD theory leads to specific and testable predictions for human and animal research. The extent to which DTD explains symptoms of ADHD and effects of pharmacological interventions is discussed. We conclude by considering the neural changes underlying the etiology of DTD. Keywords: Attention deficit hyperactivity disorder, reinforcement mechanisms, neurobiology, dopamine. Attention-deficit hyperactivity disorder (ADHD) is a debilitating condition which affects between 3 and 7% of school age children (American Psychiatric Association, 1994). It has profound adverse effects on the educational and social development of those affected. The aetiology of ADHD is unknown. It is diagnosed on the basis of persistent and develop-mentally inappropriate levels of overactivity, inat-tention and impulsivity (American Psychiatric Association, 1994). The syndrome is unlikely to have a single etiology, different subtypes are recognised clinically, and there may be a spectrum of disorders (Castellanos & Tannock, 2002). There is a strong familial genetic contribution (Biederman et al., 1992) and an association with genetic variations in the dopamine D4 receptor (Swanson et al., 2000; Swanson et al., 1998) and the dopamine transporter (Gill, Daly, Heron, Hawi, & Fitzgerald, 1997). It is likely to involve multiple genes of moderate effect. A recent review of all molecular genetic studies of ADHD from 1991 to 2004 concluded there were significant associations for four genes in ADHD: the dopamine D4 and D5 receptors, and the dopamine and serotonin transporters (Bobb, Castellanos, Addington, & Rapoport, 2006). In addition there is statistically significant evidence of association with DBH, HTR1B and SNAP-25 genes (Faraone et al., 2005). Differences in the anatomical dimensions of the caudate nucleus (Hynd et al., 1993), decreases in blood flow in regions of the striatum (Lou, Henrik-sen, Bruhn, Borner, & Nielsen, 1989), and changes in dopamine transporter binding (Dougherty et al., 1999) have been described in the human striatum in ADHD. However, current knowledge of the patho-physiology of ADHD is very limited (Castellanos & Tannock, 2002) and there is little information avail-able about the cellular mechanisms underlying this disorder. At the behavioural level there have been a number of studies that suggest that children with ADHDdiffer from other children in their response to reinforce-ment. Historically, children with ADHD have been described as less able to delay gratification and as failing to respond to discipline (Haenlein & Caul, 1987; Wender, 1971, 1972, 1974). As a group, children with ADHD have been reported to perform less well under partial schedules of reinforcement (Freibergs & Douglas, 1969; Parry & Douglas, 1983), and to respond more impulsively to reinforcements; that is, to choose small immediate reinforcement over larger delayed reinforcement (Firestone & Douglas, 1975). The human behavioural studies published between 1986 and 2003, meeting certain methodo-logical criteria, were reviewed recently by Luman, Oosterlaan & Sergeant (2005). This review identified the presence of differences in response to reinforce-ment in children with ADHD. However, it also iden-tified a lack of coherence within the existing body of literature. This is partly due to the absence of a common theoretical framework in which to reconcile diverse findings and the exploratory nature of the studies, which have not tested specific predictions. Nevertheless, there is a consistent theme that chil-dren with ADHDhave an atypical response to positive reinforcement. Recent behavioural studies of children with ADHD have extended these findings. The balance of evidence suggests that an alteration in processing of reinforcement may be a fundamental characteristic of ADHD. This characteristic has important im-plications for understanding the brain mechanisms underlying the disorder and for the development of effective behavioural and pharmacological interven-tions. Several general theories of ADHD have been pro-posed to account for its etiology, symptoms, and possible mechanisms. Some of these have included a component of reinforcement dysfunction, namely, the Cognitive-Energetic model (Sergeant, 2000), the Dual Pathway model (Sonuga-Barke, 2002, 2003), and the Dynamic Developmental Theory (Sagvolden, Johansen, Aase, & Russell, 2005). A computational model derived from recent models of dopamine cell activity has also been described (Williams & Dayan, 2005). These theories acknowledge the importance of understanding neural mechanisms of reinforcement and learning. In the present article we focus on the neural mechanisms underlying positive reinforcement and propose a neurobiological theory about how changes in these mechanisms might account for altered sensitivity to positive reinforcement in children with ADHD. Using recent neurobiological findings from animal studies regarding reinforcement-related dopamine cell activity that normally occurs in the brain, we outline the importance of the anticipatory firing of dopamine cells brought about by a transfer of dopamine cell responses to cues that precede reinforcers. We propose that in children with ADHD there are specific alterations in the magnitude and timing of this anticipatory dopamine cell firing, which we call the dopamine transfer deficit (DTD) theory. We explain how these alterations account for particular symptoms of ADHD. Specific and testable predictions are derived from the DTD theory and compared to predictions of alternative hypotheses. The neurobiological basis of DTD is discussed is some depth. Normal neurobiological mechanisms of reinforcement At the behavioural level positive reinforcement is defined in terms of effects on responses. A positive reinforcer is a stimulus that increases or maintains the likelihood that a behaviour that it follows will occur again. This behavioural effect of reinforcers is a consequence of changes occurring at the cellular level in the brain. The likelihood of a behaviour is presumably related to the strength of particular synaptic connections. This has been suggested on the basis of studies in invertebrates (Kandel & Tauc, 1965). Recently, a correlation of behavioural reinforcement and dopamine-depend-ent changes in synaptic efficacy has been demon-strated in the mammalian rat brain (Reynolds, Hyland, & Wickens, 2001). However, there is not necessarily a constant one-to-one relationship between events at the behavioural level and the neural activity occurring in the brain in relation to positive reinforcement. The processing of positive reinforcement signals in the human, non-human primate, and rat brain is now recognised as highly sophisticated, context-dependent and predictive in nature (Hassani, Cromwell, & Schultz, 2001; Schultz, 2000). In animal studies dopaminergic circuits in the midbrain, particularly the nigrostriatal and meso-limbic projections, have been identified as critical in reinforcement learning mechanisms. The intracra-nial self-stimulation (ICSS) phenomenon, in which animals learn to press a lever for electrical stimula-tion of their own brain, provided important evidence for a dopaminergic brain reward system. Dopamine release appears to be necessary for establishing ICSS (Garris et al., 1999). The highest ICSS response rates and lowest thresholds are in areas traversed by dopamine fibres or containing dopamine cell bodies (Corbett & Wise, 1980). Furthermore, dopamine antagonists attenuate the reinforcing effects of elec-trical stimulation in the most effective sites, and this attenuation is not simply due to motor side effects (Beninger & Freedman, 1982). Dopamine neurons are activated in response to natural rewards. They show phasic activations after unexpected primary rewards and conditioned, reward-predicting visual and auditory stimuli (Schultz, 1998). Dopamine cell activation in response to reward-predicting stimuli is also reflected in dopamine concentration changes (Phillips, Stuber, Heien, Wightman, & Carelli, 2003). Such changes in dopamine concentration have been shown to have reinforcing effects at the cellular level in animal studies (Arbuthnott & Wickens, 2007; Wickens, 1990; Wickens, Begg, & Arbuthnott, 1996). Collect-ively this evidence indicates that the dopamine input to the striatum is crucial for its reward-related learning functions, as summarised in recent reviews (Beninger & Miller, 1998; Wickens, Horvitz, Costa, & 692 Gail Tripp and Jeff R. Wickens  2007 The Authors Killcross, 2007; Wickens, Hyland, & Tripp, 2006; Wickens, Reynolds, & Hyland, 2003; Wise, 2006). The firing of dopamine cells in the substantia nigra and ventral tegmental area during learning involves marked plasticity. Changes in firing rate occur in relation to reinforcers and cues, their timing, and across sessions in a sophisticated way consistent with contemporary learning theories (Schultz, 2002, 2004) though not always in agreement with the simplest models (Pan, Schmidt, Wickens, & Hyland, 2005b). Briefly, in non-human primates dopamine cell firing activity has been shown to occur in response to the presentation of unexpected positive reinforcers (Ljungberg, Apicella, & Schultz, 1991; Mirenowicz & Schultz, 1994) but not to aversive events (Mirenowicz & Schultz, 1996). In some experimental situations the dopamine population activity has been shown to predict choice behaviour better than the behavioural reward (Morris, Nevet, Arkadir, Vaadia, & Bergman, 2006). Similar to the findings in non-human primates, rat dopamine cells respond to appetitive stimuli (Hyland, Reynolds, Hay, Perk, & Miller, 2002), whereas aversive events inhibit dopamine cell firing (Ungless, Magill, & Bolam, 2004). Dopamine efflux in response to visual and olfactory stimuli associated with natural rewards has also been demonstrated in the rat nucleus accumbens (Ahn & Phillips, 2002, 2007; Fiorillo, Coury, & Phillips, 1997) and striatum (Nakazato, 2005). It is now widely accepted that phasic activity of dopamine cells is related to positive reinforcers. Dopamine cell firing activity does not, however, simply slavishly follow the delivery of positive rein-forcers. A scheme illustrating changes in dopamine cell firing during learning is shown in Figure 1a. Established reinforcers, such as praise or attention, initially evoke dopamine cell firing. As the behav-iours that lead to such reinforcers become learnt, reinforcement can be obtained more reliably. As learning proceeds, dopamine cells fire in anticipation of reinforcement, that is, in response to cues that predict the delivery of reinforcement. Neuroimaging studies are consistent with the non-human primate and rat studies in showing that the human striatum is involved in reward processing. These studies have been reviewed recently (Delgado, 2007; Knutson & Cooper, 2005). Although it is not possible to differentiate specific neurotransmitters using neuroimaging methods such as functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI), pharmacological evidence suggests that dopamine release may activ-ate postsynaptic D1 receptors, which changes postsynaptic membrane potential, eventually increasing local fMRI signals (Knutson & Gibbs, 2007). Generally, activations in the striatum seem to parallel dopamine neuron activity recorded in an-imal studies. In particular, activation of the dorsal striatum seems to occur in relation to reinforcement of an action (Delgado, Miller, Inati, & Phelps, 2005; Haruno et al., 2004), and activation of the nucleus accumbens in relation to reward anticipation (Gal-van et al., 2005). Also, imaging studies in humans have shown responses to cues predicting a juice reward in ventral and dorsal striatum, with the dorsal striatum showing activity only when an active choice is required (Oâ€™Doherty et al., 2004). Thus, over the course of learning, we propose that in nor-mal children â€“ as in non-human primates and rats â€“ dopamine cell responses transfer from established reinforcers to earlier cues in the behavioural sequence. As well as signalling reinforcing events, animal studies have repeatedly shown that dopamine release (brought about by reinforcement-related activity of dopamine cells) is critical for learning on the basis of positive reinforcement (Beninger & Freedman, 1982; Beninger & Miller, 1998). The mechanism requires presynaptic activity in cortical inputs to the striatum, postsynaptic striatal cell activity, and phasic release of dopamine. This form of dopamine-dependent potentiation has been demon-strated at the synaptic level (Wickens et al., 1996) and is associated with behavioural learning (Rey-nolds et al., 2001). An important feature of the reinforcing action of dopamine at the cellular level is its strict timing requirements. Application of dopamine in a pulsatile fashion is essential (Wickens et al., 1996), and this mimicks the surge of dopamine measured in the rat brain in association with reinforcing stimulation (Cheer et al., 2007). Direct measures of the timing requirements for potentiation have shown that dopamine pulses must occur within subsecond intervals of the synaptic activity that is being potentiated (Wickens, 2000). Similar temporal requirements have been shown using dopaminergic reinforcement of single cell activity in the hippo-campus (Stein, Xue, & Belluzzi, 1994). At the cellular level dopamine has to act within a subsecond time window to produce potentiation. When predictive cue signals are not available the timing requirements for behavioural reinforcement are consistent with the timing requirements for cel-lular reinforcement. For example, when electrical stimulation of the brain is used as the reinforcer, it is possible to remove all predictive cue signals. In this situation, delay of reinforcement of as little as one second has been shown to impair the effect of rein-forcement in rats (Black, Belluzzi, & Stein, 1985). In general a delay between the response or stimu-lus and the reinforcer is known to reduce the learning-related effects of reinforcement. In particu-lar, animal studies have shown that instrumental learning is less rapid as the response-reinforcer delay increases (Critchfield & Lattal, 1993; Dickin-son, Watt, & Griffiths, 1992; Lattal & Gleeson, 1990). However, learning can be enhanced under conditions of delayed reinforcement by a distinctive environmental cue, which reliably precedes delivery of the final reinforcer. Such a cue can become associated with reinforcement, and act as a condi-tioned reinforcer. The conditioned reinforcer can be thought of as â€˜bridgingâ€™ the delay, and providing immediate secondary reinforcement (Cardinal, Win-stanley, Robbins, & Everitt, 2004; Grice, 1948). Cues presented during the delay interval facilitate learning (Renner, 1964; Tarpy & Sawabini, 1974). In natural learning situations the organismâ€™s response, or the delay itself, can act as a predictive cue or conditioned reinforcer (Ferster, 1953; Garrud, Goo-dall, & Mackintosh, 1981; Winstanley, Theobald, Cardinal, & Robbins, 2004). The cellular mechanisms underlying this bridging effect of conditioned reinforcers may involve the transfer of dopamine cell responses from established reinforcers to earlier cues after pairings of the cue with the reinforcer, as outlined above. This transfer of the dopamine cell firing to earlier cues predicting reinforcement explains how behaviour can be maintained in situations where external reinforce-ment is not immediate. Anticipatory firing of the dopamine cells ensures that the dopamine pulses occur with the required timing at the cellular level even if the external reinforcer is delayed or discon-tinuous. Thus, the anticipatory dopamine cell firing is the neural basis of conditioned reinforcement, and similar mechanisms are probable in humans. A specific theory of altered reinforcement mechanisms in ADHD Our theory of altered reinforcement mechanisms in children with ADHD is based on several key postu-lates: 1 In normal children, the dopamine cell response to positive reinforcement transfers to previously neutral cues that predict reinforcement (as illus-trated in Figure 1a). 2 In normal children the transfer of dopamine cell responses to cues that predict reinforcement provides an anticipatory dopamine signal that delivers immediate and continuous cellular rein-forcement when behavioural reinforcement is delayed or discontinuous. 3 In children with ADHD, the transfer of the dopa-mine response to previously neutral cues is dis-rupted. In particular, the transfer of the dopamine cell response to the cue that predicts reinforcement fails to develop normally, so that the anticipatory dopamine signal is weak or non-existent (as illustrated in Figure 1b). 4 In children with ADHD, when behavioural rein-forcement is delayed or discontinuous, the failure to produce an anticipatory dopamine signal leads to delayed or discontinuous dopamine signalling and ineffective reinforcement at the cellular level. 5 In normal children responding is maintained when reinforcement is discontinued because of contin-ued anticipatory dopamine release, until the latter is extinguished. In children with ADHD the absent or reduced anticipatory dopamine release leads to more rapid extinction of the behavioural response. These postulates together form the dopamine-transfer deficit (DTD) theory of altered sensitivity to reinforcement in ADHD. DTD and delay of reinforcement Behaviourally, there are many situations in which there is a delay in the delivery of reinforcers. As no-ted above, such delays are known to affect learning on the basis of reinforcement. In general, learning in animals occurs more slowly when there is delay of reinforcement (Renner, 1964). With longer delays, reinforcement is less effective; a phenomenon termed the delay of reinforcement gradient (Catania, 2005). Many factors contribute to the course of this gradi-ent. Importantly, the effects of delays are reduced by cues presented during the delay interval. The DTD theory assumes that in normal children, the transfer of the dopamine cell response to such cues provides an anticipatory dopamine signal that bridges the delay. This transfer, however, takes time to develop. Also, non-human primate studies suggest that the anticipatory cell firing response is smaller in mag-nitude than the response to the established rein-forcer (Mirenowicz & Schultz, 1994). These aspects of the transfer of dopamine signalling may explain the normal ability of children to learn even when reinforcement is delayed, and why the rate of learn-ing is slower under conditions of delayed reinforce-ment. The DTD theory predicts that for children with ADHD, there is a dopamine transfer dysfunction. In particular, the phasic dopamine cell response to the cue that predicts reinforcement is reduced in amplitude to the point of being ineffective, although the timing of this cue is normal. The phasic dopa-mine cell response occurs after the positive rein-forcer is delivered. Thus, for children with ADHD, under conditions of delayed reinforcement, the phasic dopamine cell response is also delayed, with consequently reduced anticipatory delivery of cellu-lar reinforcement under conditions of delayed behavioural reinforcement. Under such conditions, children with ADHD would experience a delayed dopamine signal, rather than the immediate anti-cipatory dopamine signal that normal children experience. Under these conditions learning would be slower, and may even fail to occur. In the absence of an anticipatory dopamine signal even short delays are likely to influence the effect-iveness of reinforcement. Tripp and Alsop (2001) demonstrated that a delay as short as 3.5 seconds differentially affected the behaviour of children with and without ADHD. Altered responding on a fixed- interval schedule has been interpreted as a steeper delay of reinforcement gradient, operating over a similar timescale of seconds (Sagvolden, Aase, Zeiner, & Berger, 1998). These timescales are com-patible with animal studies in which intracranial reinforcement is used (which ensures that there are no predictive cues), where delays on the order of a second or so impair the effects of reinforcement (Black et al., 1985). In contrast, when there is a cue that can signal an upcoming reward (and hence evoke an anticipatory dopamine release), delay of reinforcement has a gradual effect depending on the activity of the subject during the delay period (Ren-ner, 1964). The delay in the dopamine signal would also have other consequences. For example, in children with ADHD, if the target behavior is not externally rein-forced immediately then, unlike normal children, there is no anticipatory dopamine signal. This results in failure to reinforce the target behaviour, and creates an opportunity for competing behav-iours to occur, which may be reinforced by other events. This may lead to children with ADHD engaging in off-task behaviours that do result in immediate reinforcement. DTD and partial reinforcement In normal children, partial reinforcement, in which not every response is reinforced, also slows learning (Cunningham & Knights, 1978; Freibergs & Doug-las, 1969; Parry & Douglas, 1983). The DTD theory assumes that under conditions of partial reinforce-ment anticipatory dopamine cell activity provides continuous cellular reinforcement in normal children. In other words, there is reliable dopamine cell firing in the absence of behavioural reinforce-ment. This is analogous to the proposed role of bridging cues in delayed reinforcement. The DTD theory predicts that under conditions of partial reinforcement children with ADHD will fail to acquire the anticipatory dopamine cell activity that provides continuous cellular reinforcement in nor-mal children. This might explain why the behaviour of children with ADHD sometimes differs under partial schedules of reinforcement, but is indistin-guishable from that of controls when reinforcement is continuous (Freibergs & Douglas, 1969; Parry & Douglas, 1983). Under continuous reinforcement, children with ADHD â€“ like normal children â€“ expe-rience continuous reinforcement at the cellular level, as this is not disrupted by failure of the transfer of the dopamine signal. However, under partial rein-forcement, normal children experience continuous reinforcement at the cellular level, while children with ADHD will experience partial reinforcement at the cellular as well as at the behavioural level. Children with ADHD will therefore take longer than controls to learn under conditions of partial Dopamine transfer deficit in ADHD 695  2007 The Authors reinforcement, and after learning, their performance will be less secure. Under continuous reinforcement DTD does not differentiate between children with and without ADHD, because every instance of target behaviour is reinforced. As with delay of reinforcement, as discussed above, under schedules of partial reinforcement there is opportunity for other sources of reinforce-ment or stimulation to disrupt performance. Another important aspect of learning under partial reinforcement is that such learning is more resistant to extinction. This is known as the partial rein-forcement extinction effect, or PREE (Myers, 1960). The partial reinforcement extinction effect has been extensively studied in animals but there is little experimental evidence from humans as yet. The DTD theory assumes that during extinction, in normal children the conditioned anticipatory release of dopamine continues until the dopamine response is extinguished. In rats, when tested in extinction, dopamine is released in response to a conditioned stimulus during the initial trials, but not later ones (Ahn & Phillips, 2007). Conversely, much evidence suggests that extinction of behavioural responses will occur if they are performed in the absence of a phasic dopamine signal. For example, dopamine antagonists produce an extinction-like decline in operant responding (Beninger et al., 1987; Wise & Rompre, 1989). This extinction-like effect is selective for the operant behaviour performed during dopa-mine receptor blockade (Beninger & Freedman, 1982), which is why not all behaviours extinguish. Thus, extinction of the dopamine response leads eventually to the extinction of behavioural responses. In support of this proposal, PREE is dopamine dependent. In rats, dopamine antagonists given during extinction facilitate extinction and block PREE (Feldon, Katz, & Weiner, 1988). The DTD theory predicts that in children with ADHD, the absence of anticipatory dopamine under partial reinforcement should lead to more rapid extinction of desired behaviours and a diminished or absent PREE. DTD and integration of reinforcement history At a higher level of behavioural analysis the organ-ismâ€™s experience is not simply determined by the immediate behavioural contingencies but also by its internal representation of those contingencies (Killeen & Sitomer, 2003). In normal children, pre-vious experiences with reinforcement are integrated in an internal model, which lessens the impact of individual instances of reinforcement. This integra-tion of reinforcement history over time serves to strengthen on-task or preferred behaviours, that is, normal children develop an adaptive representation of contingencies. Normal children thus select those actions that typically result in reinforcement, over those that most recently resulted in reinforcement (Tripp & Alsop, 1999b). The DTD theory proposes that integration of rein-forcement history over time is a function of the shift in dopamine cell response to cues that predict rein-forcement. In this context, the transfer of the dopa-mine cell response is a mechanism for giving greater emphasis to the longer-term history of reinforce-ment, relative to individual instances of reinforce-ment. We define the longer-term history of reinforcement as the â€˜globalâ€™ contingencies, and the individual instances as â€˜localâ€™. The global versus local distinction is based on the time scale over which the effect of past instances of reinforcement affect behaviour. The processing of global contin-gencies is likely to involve neural systems that are afferent to the dopamine cells, and include brain areas such as the prefrontal cortex, amygdala, and nucleus accumbens. Determining the global contin-gencies is a higher integrative function that may involve working memory. Dopamine cell activity is the outcome of this activity and thus represents an internal model of the reinforcement contingencies operating in the external environment. Failure of dopamine transfer would lead to increased sensitivity to local or recent instances of reinforcement, due to a smaller anticipatory dopa-mine cell response and a relatively larger response to actual instances of reinforcement. Behaviourally, the DTD theory predicts that in children with ADHD the smaller anticipatory dopamine signal relative to the response to actual reinforcers would result in individual instances of actual reinforcement having undue influence over behaviour. This has been demonstrated by Tripp and Alsop (1999b). A trial-by-trial analysis showed that children with ADHD were more sensitive to small, local changes in reinforce-ment than control children, who were more influ-enced by global contingencies. The heightened sensitivity to individual instances of reinforcement means that children with ADHD are more strongly influenced by the reinforcement con-tingencies in their immediate environment and less influenced by an internal model of global contin-gencies than normal children. This could explain the reported lack of self-control of children with ADHD. It may also explain why attention is drawn off task. The childâ€™s off-task behaviour might be a response to the operation of local rather than global contingen-cies of reinforcement. Similarly, the impulsivity of children with ADHD could be interpreted as sensi-tivity to local rather than global reinforcement con-tingencies. DTD and the effects of methylphenidate The symptom-reducing effects of methylphenidate in ADHD are widely documented. However, the precise mechanism by which methylphenidate exerts these 696 Gail Tripp and Jeff R. Wickens  2007 The Authors therapeutic effects is not known. In this paper we focus on the dopaminergic effects of methylpheni-date, as these are most relevant to DTD. At the cellular level methylphenidate acts to potentiate dopamine signalling by increasing the effect of dopamine cell firing on the synaptic concentration of dopamine. The DTD theory predicts that the anticipatory dopamine signal is reduced in ampli-tude to the point of being ineffective in children with ADHD. Methylphenidate, by increasing the synaptic concentration of dopamine, may act in a compen-satory way, by increasing the size of the anticipatory dopamine cell response to predictive cues, although not necessarily to normal levels. In support of this prediction it has been shown in animal studies that methylphenidate increases the efficacy of conditioned reinforcers (Robbins, 1975, 1978), as originally suggested by Hill (1970). It has not been determined if this is due to amplifying dopamine release in response to cues. However, whatever the neural mechanism of this effect, at the behavioural level Wade et al. (2000) have shown that D-amphetamine, which has similar effects to methylphenidate, increases preference for delayed reinforcers. In rats, consistent with the DTD theory, Cardinal, Robbins and Everitt (2000) showed that this increased preference for delayed reinforcers occurred only when the delay was signalled. These findings are consistent with facilitation of the response to predictive cues by methylphenidate. In the context of the DTD theory, the facilitation of the response to predictive cues by methylphenidate suggests a possible basis for its therapeutic effects in children with ADHD. Methylphenidate should reduce the effect of delay of reinforcement by amplifying the effects of â€˜bridgingâ€™ cues. Methyl-phenidate should also enhance learning under con-ditions of partial reinforcement, by a similar mechanism. By increasing the anticipatory dopa-mine signal relative to the response to actual rein-forcers, methylphenidate should also reduce the effect that individual instances of actual reinforce-ment have over behaviour. To our knowledge only the last of these predictions has been studied. In children with ADHD Tripp and Alsop (1999b) demonstrated that methylphenidate reduced but did not normalise the effects of indi-vidual instances of reinforcement on behaviour. Comparison of DTD with previous models In common with this proposal, several other models have suggested altered firing patterns of dopamine neurons in ADHD like that shown in Figure 1. In particular, Sagvolden et al. (2005) propose that the transfer of the dopamine signal occurs more slowly in ADHD. Williams and Dayan (2005) similarly pro-pose a decreased rate at which predictions change in response to a prediction error. These assumptions are in agreement with DTD. However, DTD makes a number of unique predictions that are experimen-tally testable and distinct from those of Sagvolden et al. (2005) and Williams and Dayan (2005). Both Sagvolden et al. (2005) and Williams and Dayan (2005) argue that there should be deficient extinction of previously reinforced behaviour, specifically arguing for slower rates of extinction in such a system. In contrast, the DTD theory predicts the opposite, namely a faster rate of extinction. This prediction is based on the assumed absence of an anticipatory dopamine signal at the onset of extinc-tion. In normal children, the anticipatory dopamine signal would be expected to continue in extinction, as occurs in rat dopamine neurons during extinction of classically conditioned stimulusâ€“reward associ-ations (Pan, Schmidt, Wickens, & Hyland, 2005a). In normal children such a signal would maintain performance in the initial stages of extinction. In children with ADHD, however, the absence of the anticipatory dopamine signal would mean that behaviour was not being reinforced at the cellular level, predicting more rapid extinction. In animal studies, the facilitation of extinction by dopamine antagonists given during extinction (Feldon et al., 1988) is consistent with this interpretation. At pres-ent, there is no human data to test this prediction. Another important difference concerns the effects of methylphenidate. Williams and Dayan (2005) assume that methylphenidate acts by increasing â€˜learning rateâ€™, a parameter in their model that rep-resents the size of the effect of prediction errors on changes in predictions. The faster learning rate would be expected to lead to more rapid extinction. In contrast, DTD assumes that methylphenidate acts by amplifying the dopamine response to previously neutral cues that have become predictors of rein-forcement. This would lead to more effective rein-forcement at the cellular level in response to cues that predict reward, and would lead to greater resistance to extinction. Thus, this assumption leads to predictions about the effects of methylphenidate on extinction that are mutually exclusive to the predictions of Williams and Dayan. Related to the effect on extinction, DTD also pre-dicts that PREE (Myers, 1960) will be reduced or absent in children with ADHD. Children with ADHD are assumed to fail to acquire the anticipatory release of dopamine under conditions of partial reinforcement. The absence of this anticipatory release leads to a similar state as that brought about by dopamine antagonists. When dopamine antagon-ists are applied during extinction there is a reduced PREE (Feldon et al., 1988). Thus, DTD predicts that children with ADHD should show reduced or absent PREE. It is important to note that Williams and Dayan (2005) modelled impulsivity in a delayed response time task in which a small reinforcement is given for immediate action and a larger reinforcement for a Dopamine transfer deficit in ADHD 697  2007 The Authors delayed action. In the behavioural task that they model the response-reinforcer delay is the same for either choice of action, but one choice is not available until after a delay. The prediction of their model was consistent with the delay aversion hypothesis (Sonuga-Barke, Taylor, Sembi, & Smith, 1992), that children with ADHD are averse to waiting per se. In contrast the present model predicts a specific pref-erence for immediate over delayed reinforcement, i.e., it focuses on the delay between the response and the subsequent reinforcement. In this respect the models address different questions and their pre-dictions are not mutually exclusive. Although it is important to identify the predictions of these different models at the behavioural level, another crucial test is to compare assumptions and predictions at the neurochemical and cellular levels. Sagvolden et al. and Williams and Dayan envisage in the normal case that there is a gradual shift in the timing of the dopamine signal between the time of the cue and the reinforcer. This is a common assumption of classical temporal-difference learning models, but one that is not seen in neurophysiolog-ical recordings made from dopamine cells in awake rats over the course of learning (Pan et al., 2005b). In these experiments the dopamine signal appears as a new response to the cue as learning proceeds, with-out appearing at intermediate time points between the cue and the reinforcer. Other neurobiological assumptions of the models need to be put to the test, both in humans with ADHD and in animal models. For example, Sagvolden et al. hypothesise a â€˜hypodopaminergicâ€™ state in ADHD. We argue that such a state should affect the response to established reinforcers as well as conditioned rein-forcers. To the contrary, we predict that established reinforcers will evoke normal release of dopamine in children with ADHD, whereas conditioned reinforc-ers will fail to evoke dopamine release. Another test-able predication is whether methylphenidate produces the effects envisaged on dopaminergic responses to cues. This is testable in animal models using electrophysiological or electrochemical ap-proaches. Tests in human participants are less conclusive because of the difficulty of measuring dopaminergic activity by non-invasive methods. However, a recent study using fMRI in human ado-lescents with ADHD demonstrated reduced activa-tion of the ventral striatum in reward anticipation relative to controls (Scheres, Milham, Knutson, & Castellanos, 2007). In this study, participants were presented with a cue that signalled the opportunity to win money, or avoid losing money, by responding with a button press. In control trials a button press was also required, but the cues signalled no money would be won or lost. Consistent with dopamine cell firing activity in primates, control participants showed activation of the ventral striatum in the period after cues that signalled gain. This activation did not occur in response to non-reward cues. In participants with ADHD, there was no significant anticipatory activation of striatal regions after cues that signalled gain. Even though the ADHD parti-cipants in these experiments had explicit knowledge of what the cues predicted, they did not show anti-cipatory activation of striatal regions. Also, their neural responses to the outcome were not signifi-cantly different from control participants. These findings are consistent with the DTD hypothesis in all respects. Although it is not possible to say with certainty that the BOLD signal measured using this technique corresponds to dopamine release, there evidence that it is correlated with dopamine release or dopa-mine action at the dopamine receptors implicated in synaptic plasticity (Dixon et al., 2005). In the foregoing we have developed the DTD theory in order to explain reported alterations in processing of positive reinforcement in children with ADHD. This theory details the neural mechanisms underlying the observed differences in reinforcement processes in children with and without ADHD. A number of specific predictions regarding the expected behaviour of children with ADHD in response to different rein-forcement schedules were derived from this theory. These predictions are summarised in Table 1. Further work is needed to formalise the DTD the-ory as a computational model and examine its predications for the effects of DTD on learning under different schedules of reinforcement. A number of the DSM IV symptoms of inattention may be interpreted as altered behavioural control, specifically due to failure of anticipatory dopamine cell firing, leading to control by local rather than global reinforcement contingencies. These symptoms include: â€˜often fails to give close attention to details or makes careless mistakes in schoolwork, work, or other activitiesâ€™; â€˜often has difficulty sustaining attention in tasks or play activitiesâ€™ and â€˜is often easily distracted by extraneous stimuliâ€™. In terms of the DTD theory, these symptoms of â€˜inattentionâ€™ are off-task behaviours that occur with higher frequency in children with ADHD. In normal children, on-task behaviour (â€˜attendingâ€™) is maintained because they are being continually reinforced for attending by anticipatory dopamine release. This anticipatory dopamine release develops in normal children because their dopamine system is able to use previ-ous instances of reinforced behaviour to produce anticipatory dopamine release. In children with ADHD the failure to develop anticipatory dopamine release results in a lack of dopamine cell activity in response to attending. Dopamine release occurs only in response to actual instances of reinforcement. These may be provided by the environment, but in the absence of continual supervision, other aspects of the environment reinforce off-task behaviours. For example, this might include the attention of other classmates to their off-task behaviour, or novel stimuli unrelated to target activities. For example, a child looking out the window may see a truck going past, and that serves to reinforce the behaviour of looking out the window. Thus, off task behaviours that have the features of DSM IV symptoms of â€˜inattentionâ€™ occur with higher frequency in children with ADHD. Other DSM IV symptoms of inattention for which the DTD theory is relevant include â€˜often does not follow through on instructions and fails to finish schoolwork, chores, or duties in the workplaceâ€™ and â€˜often avoids, dislikes, or is reluctant to engage in tasks that require sustained mental effortâ€™. These symptoms can be interpreted as failure of condi-tioned reinforcers leading to increased sensitivity to delay of reinforcement and less effective performance under schedules of partial reinforcement. In contrast, the DTD theory has less explanatory power for the remaining DSM IV symptoms of inat-tention, namely â€˜often does not seem to listen when spoken to directlyâ€™, â€˜often has difficulty organising tasks and activitiesâ€™, â€˜often loses things necessary for tasks or activitiesâ€™ and â€˜often forgetful in daily activ-itiesâ€™. These symptoms appear to involve other pri-mary psychological processes such as internal representation of contingencies, which may not be dopamine dependent, although the output of these processes may indirectly involve reinforcement mechanisms. The DTD theory also explains some DSM IV symptoms of hyperactivity and impulsivity. The symptom of â€˜often leaves seat in classroom or in other situations in which remaining seated is expectedâ€™ may be interpreted as a lack of effective reinforcement for remaining seated, due to smaller activity of dopamine neurons, because of a deficit in anticipation of reinforcement. The symptoms of â€˜often has difficulty awaiting turnâ€™, â€˜often blurts out answers before questions have been completedâ€™, and â€˜often interrupts or intrudes on othersâ€™ may also be explained by the DTD theory, since these symptoms involve a delay between the target behaviour and the actual reinforcement. Therefore, they may be inter-preted as due to abnormal sensitivity to delay of reinforcement arising from a failure of the transfer of dopamine cell firing activity in response to cues that predict reinforcement. The remaining DSM IV symptoms of hyperactivity and impulsivity do not lend themselves to obvious interpretation in terms of the DTD theory. These are â€˜often fidgets with hands or feet or squirms in seatâ€™, â€˜often runs about or climbs excessively in situations in which it is inappropriateâ€™, â€˜often has difficulty playing or engaging in leisure activities quietlyâ€™, â€˜is often â€˜â€˜on the goâ€™â€™ or acts as if â€˜â€˜driven by a motorâ€™â€™â€™, and â€˜often talks excessivelyâ€™. These symptoms may, however, involve other consequences of altered dopamine signalling. For example, dopamine has motor activating effects due to effects on excitability of striatal neurons, as well as reinforcing actions (Miller, Wickens, & Beninger, 1990; Wickens, 1990). If the DTD theory is correct, then there may be a specific condition with several symptoms of ADHD that can be attributed to a deficit in the transfer of dopamine signals as we have outlined. However, it would be possible to have DTD and not meet criteria for ADHD, based on the DSM IV requirement of a minimum of six symptoms of inattention and/or hyperactivity-impulsivity (DTD accounts for only five or four, respectively). Nevertheless, by definition DTD is predicted to be responsive to methylpheni-date, and to produce behavioural dysfunction in situations where self-control is required (such as school). DTD may, therefore, biologically characte-rise a core component of the more broadly defined syndrome of ADHD. Etiology of neurobiological changes underlying DTD The neurobiological mechanisms that underlie the transfer of dopamine signalling are in the process of being defined. At this stage most work has concen-trated on demonstrating that such transfers take place in humans and animals during learning and choice behaviour. Correlational studies have Dopamine transfer deficit in ADHD 699  2007 The Authors revealed brain activity in specific regions that appears to be associated with anticipation of rein-forcement. However, there is limited experimental understanding of the mechanisms underlying these associations. Such understanding requires manip-ulation of the various nuclei that may contribute. Nevertheless, there is good convergence of animal and human findings with respect to the anatomical regions involved in processing of delayed or uncer-tain reinforcement. Lesion studies in animals have identified several brain regions as playing a key role in processing delay of reinforcement (see Figure 2). The basolateral amygdala may contribute to conditioned reinforce-ment by maintaining a representation of the appetit-ive value of the conditioned stimulus (Cador, Robbins, & Everitt, 1989). Consistent with the role proposed for bridging delay, Winstanley et al. (2004) showed that lesions of the basolateral amygdala in-creased sensitivity to delay of reinforcement. Thus, the basolateral amygdala is a site of interest to DTD. The nucleus accumbens also plays a role in con-ditioned behavioural activation, and in making the appropriate discriminated response. In rats, nucleus accumbens dopamine depletion impairs both acquisition and performance of appetitive approach behaviour (Parkinson et al., 2002). Consistent with this, lesions of the nucleus accumbens core were found to induce a deficit during delayed reinforce-ment but not when reinforcement was immediate (Cardinal, Pennicott, Sugathapala, Robbins, & Eve-ritt, 2001; Cardinal et al., 2004). The role of the orbitofrontal cortex is more complicated. Winstanley et al. (2004) argue that the orbitofrontal cortex is involved in revising the rep-resentation of the incentive value of a response as the outcome associated with that response changes, based on Schoenbaum, Setlow, Saddoris, and Gallagher (2003). The examples given above illustrate some of the brain regions that are implicated in processing of reinforcement, though this is not an exhaustive list. In view of the many brain regions involved we presume there are multiple mechanisms which could play a role in altered transfer of dopamine signalling. Dis-orders that affect the structures that provide input to the dopamine cells, related to the representation of conditioned reinforcers, would be expected to produce DTD. These include the structures referred to above, which are already implicated in sensitivity to delay of reinforcement and partial reinforcement in animal studies. These structures could be affected by exposure to environmental toxins, complications of encephalitis, and neonatal hypoxia. While there may be multiple routes to DTD, not all disorders of dopamine signalling would be predicted to produce the symptoms of DTD. Pathophysiological changes that affect dopamine release, reuptake or dopamine receptors would be predicted to produce more general reinforcement deficits, rather than the selective effects on response to different schedules of reinforcement predicted by DTD. For example, decreased dopamine release under all conditions would affect continuous reinforcement as much as partial reinforcement. This might lead to decreased sensitivity to reinforcement contingencies even under continuous reinforcement as opposed to a more selective failure to integrate reinforcement history (Tripp & Alsop, 1999a), an increased prefer-ence for immediate over delayed reinforcement (Tripp & Alsop, 2001), or selective deficit under partial reinforcement schedules.
